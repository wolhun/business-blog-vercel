Step-by-Step Guide to Complying with EU, UK, and US Supplement Regulations

By Ethan Reynolds 08 December 2025 · 5 min read

What Are the Key Differences in Supplement Regulations Across the EU, UK, and US?

The European Union (EU) regulates dietary supplements under the Food Supplements Directive (2002/46/EC), which sets out specific composition requirements and labeling standards. Supplements must be safe, properly labeled, and not misleading. The directive mandates that only vitamins and minerals listed in Annex I can be used, limiting the range of ingredients in supplements.

In contrast, the United Kingdom (UK) follows similar regulations to the EU with the Food Safety Act 1990 and the Nutrition and Health Claims Regulation (EC) No 1924/2006. Post-Brexit, the UK has the authority to diverge from EU regulations. The UK focuses on safety and claims substantiation, requiring that health claims made on labels are authorized and based on scientific evidence.

The United States (US) operates under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994. Unlike the EU and UK, the US allows a broader range of ingredients without pre-market approval. Manufacturers are responsible for ensuring the safety and labeling of their products. The FDA can intervene post-marketing if a product is found to be unsafe or improperly labeled.

How to Determine Which Regulations Apply to Your Supplement Product?

1. Identify the Ingredients

Start by listing all the ingredients in your supplement. Each ingredient may have specific regulations based on its classification. For example, vitamins and minerals typically fall under food supplement regulations, while herbal extracts may be subject to additional scrutiny.

2. Evaluate the Intended Use

Define the intended use of your supplement. Is it marketed for general health, specific health claims, or as a performance enhancer? The claims associated with the product significantly influence the regulatory framework. Health claims may require pre-market approval in certain jurisdictions.

3. Research Relevant Regulations

Familiarize yourself with the regulations in your target markets. The EU, UK, and US have different requirements for dietary supplements. For instance, the EU mandates compliance with the Food Supplements Directive, while the US follows the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA). Understanding these regulations is crucial for compliance.

4. Consult Regulatory Experts

Engage with regulatory experts or consultants who specialize in dietary supplements. They can provide insights into compliance strategies, potential pitfalls, and the latest regulatory updates. Their expertise can be invaluable in navigating complex regulations.

5. Document Everything

Maintain thorough documentation of your product development process, including ingredient sourcing, intended use, and regulatory assessments. This documentation serves as evidence of compliance and may be required during inspections or audits by regulatory bodies.

Essential Steps for Compliance in the EU, UK, and US

1. Identify the relevant directives and regulations for your region.

Understanding the specific directives and regulations is crucial for compliance. In the EU, the Food Supplements Directive (2002/46/EC) outlines the framework for dietary supplements. The UK follows similar guidelines post-Brexit, while the US adheres to the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA). Familiarizing yourself with these regulations ensures adherence to local laws.

2. Prepare the necessary documentation for product registration.

Documentation varies by region. In the EU, submit a notification to the appropriate authority, often requiring product specifications and safety data. The UK mandates similar documentation, while the US requires a New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) notification for certain products. Collecting accurate information expedites the registration process.

3. Ensure compliance with labeling requirements.

Labeling standards differ significantly. The EU requires labels to include specific nutrient information and health claims substantiated by scientific evidence. The UK has retained many EU labeling rules but may have distinct requirements post-Brexit. In the US, labels must comply with FDA guidelines, including ingredient lists and nutritional facts. Adhering to these regulations prevents misbranding issues.

4. Conduct safety assessments and risk evaluations.

Safety assessments are mandatory. The EU expects comprehensive risk evaluations, including toxicological data and efficacy studies. The UK follows similar protocols, ensuring products do not pose health risks. In the US, manufacturers must ensure that their products are safe and meet FDA standards. Conducting thorough assessments protects consumer health and maintains compliance.

5. Understand the specific requirements for the UK post-Brexit.

Post-Brexit, the UK has established its regulatory framework. Companies must register products with the UK Food Standards Agency (FSA), and compliance with local labeling laws is essential. Understanding these changes is vital for maintaining market access and ensuring product viability.

6. Navigate FDA regulations for dietary supplements in the US.

The FDA enforces specific regulations for dietary supplements. Manufacturers must ensure products are safe, properly labeled, and compliant with Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP). Understanding these regulations helps avoid penalties, ensuring that products meet safety and quality standards.

What Common Pitfalls Should You Avoid When Complying with Supplement Regulations?

  • Neglecting Labeling Requirements: Companies often overlook specific labeling rules, leading to non-compliance. Ensure all ingredients, nutritional information, and health claims meet regulatory standards.

  • Inadequate Documentation: Failing to maintain comprehensive records of product formulations, testing, and compliance checks can result in significant penalties. Keep detailed logs to demonstrate adherence to regulations.

  • Ignoring Local Regulations: Different jurisdictions have unique requirements. Companies may assume that compliance in one region suffices for others. Research and understand local regulations in every market.

  • Overstating Health Claims: Many businesses mistakenly make exaggerated health claims. Regulatory bodies scrutinize such statements. Ensure claims are supported by scientific evidence to avoid legal repercussions.

  • Insufficient Testing: Skipping necessary safety and efficacy tests can lead to unsafe products. Conduct thorough testing to ensure that supplements are both safe and effective before market entry.

  • Failing to Update Compliance Practices: Regulations can change. Companies often neglect to review and update their compliance practices regularly. Stay informed about regulatory changes to maintain compliance.

  • Relying Solely on Third-Party Certifications: Trusting third-party certifications without conducting internal checks can be risky. Verify that all certifications align with current regulations and standards.

How to Stay Updated on Regulatory Changes in the Supplement Industry?

Monitoring regulatory updates is crucial for maintaining compliance in the supplement industry across the EU, UK, and US. Subscribing to official government websites is an effective strategy. Websites such as the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), the UK Food Standards Agency (FSA), and the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) provide timely updates on regulatory changes. These platforms typically offer newsletters or alerts that can keep you informed about new guidelines and amendments.

Engaging with industry associations can also enhance your awareness of regulatory developments. Organizations such as the Council for Responsible Nutrition (CRN) and the European Federation of Associations of Health Product Manufacturers (EHPM) frequently publish reports and insights regarding compliance issues. Attendance at trade shows and conferences organized by these associations can foster networking opportunities, allowing you to share experiences and learn from others facing similar regulatory challenges.
